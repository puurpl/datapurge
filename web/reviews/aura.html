Aura review

Orange - trade-offs Reviewed July 2026

Aura Sub, LLC (VC-backed) · Boston, US · formed 2019

Bottom line

Aura isn't really a data-removal service - it's a broad, polished identity-protection suite (credit monitoring, VPN, antivirus, password manager, insurance) that includes broker removal on every plan. If you want an all-in-one kit and don't mind handing over a lot of personal data, it's a legitimate, well-reviewed option and it is not itself a data broker. But for removal specifically it's a compromise: it requires your SSN, it disclosed a roughly 900,000-record breach in March 2026, it draws a lot of billing complaints, and there's no independent test of its removal effectiveness.

Who owns it - and can you trust them?

Aura (legal entity Aura Sub, LLC) was formed in 2019 by merging the old credit-monitoring firm Intersections Inc. with the startup iSubscribed, backed by the venture firms WndrCo and General Catalyst. On the core "both ends" question it's clean: Aura doesn't operate data-broker or people-search sites - it acts as your opt-out agent, not a data seller.

Two pieces of background for the full picture: Aura's CEO, Hari Ravichandran, personally settled SEC charges in 2018 (from his earlier company, Endurance International) over allegations of overstating subscriber numbers - a $1.38M settlement without admitting wrongdoing, unrelated to Aura. And Aura's own privacy policy discloses advertising data-sharing that California law classifies as a "sale/share." Neither is disqualifying, but both belong on the record.

Security & the 2026 breach

Aura disclosed a ~900,000-record breach in March 2026

An attacker compromised an Aura employee's account through a targeted voice-phishing (vishing) attack - publicly attributed to the ShinyHunters group - and accessed roughly 900,000 records from a marketing database tied to Circle Media Labs (a company Aura acquired in 2021). Aura says no Social Security numbers, passwords, or financial data were exposed; the leaked fields were names, addresses, phone numbers, emails, and support notes. For a company whose entire brand is protecting your identity, it's a notable incident - though it hit a marketing list, not the core protection platform.

Aura claims SOC 2 Type II and ISO 27001 compliance (self-reported; we couldn't corroborate a separate PCI-DSS claim it has made). Given the March 2026 event, treat the marketing security badges with the usual pinch of salt.

Your privacy & data

This is the biggest trade-off. Because Aura bundles credit monitoring and identity-theft insurance, it collects far more than a removal-only tool needs - name, date of birth, and your Social Security Number (or government ID), plus billing and, for some features, uploaded documents. If your goal is simply to get off people-search sites, that's a lot of sensitive data to concentrate in one company - especially one that just had a breach. It does state it doesn't sell your data, with the California ad-tech caveat noted above.

Does it actually work?

Aura wasn't included in Consumer Reports' controlled study, so there's no independent measurement of its removal effectiveness specifically. It covers 200+ sites and re-checks periodically, and it's well-reviewed overall for breadth and polish (U.S. News ranked it #2 for identity-theft protection in 2026) - but that's a whole-suite ranking, not a test of the removal feature. It also draws significant billing and cancellation complaints (a very low BBB customer-review score despite an A+ accreditation grade), a recurring theme worth weighing.

Pros

  • Removal bundled in every tier; not itself a broker
  • Broad suite: credit, VPN, antivirus, insurance
  • Well-reviewed for breadth and ease of use
  • 60-day guarantee and 14-day trial

Cons

  • Requires SSN/ID - a large data footprint to concentrate
  • March 2026 breach (~900k records)
  • Heavy billing/cancellation complaints
  • No independent removal-effectiveness data
