OneRep review

Red - avoid Reviewed July 2026

Founder-owned (Dimitri Shelest) · registered in Virginia; operated from Belarus/Cyprus

Bottom line

OneRep is the clearest "fox guarding the henhouse" case in this whole category, and it's the reason your instinct about "data processor owners making money at both ends" is exactly the right question to ask. In March 2024, KrebsOnSecurity documented that OneRep's founder and CEO, Dimitri Shelest, had spent years building dozens of people-search sites - and, in his own on-the-record response, acknowledged still holding an ownership stake in one of them (Nuwber). That's the exact category of site OneRep charges you to escape. Mozilla, which had bundled OneRep into its Monitor Plus product, ended the relationship over it. The service may technically work for many users, but on the trust question that matters most here, we say avoid it.

Who owns it - and can you trust them?

This is the entire story. OneRep is owned and run by its founder, Dimitri Shelest. In March 2024, investigative reporter Brian Krebs published a detailed investigation at KrebsOnSecurity showing that an email address tied to Shelest was associated with the registration of roughly 179 domain names - most of them people-search sites, including Nuwber, targeting people in many countries.

The founder runs both sides

OneRep's own website stated that it "is not associated with Nuwber.com" - yet, responding to the reporting on the record, Shelest acknowledged founding Nuwber and said he continues to "maintain an ownership stake" in it (while claiming "zero cross-over or information-sharing with OneRep"). In other words: the same person profits from a business that lists your personal data and from the service that charges you to remove it. KrebsOnSecurity also reported that OneRep affiliate links have appeared on data-broker opt-out pages (e.g. ClustrMaps), creating a loop where opting out can indirectly funnel money back to the broker ecosystem.

Mozilla ended its partnership over it

Mozilla had used OneRep to power its paid Monitor Plus removal service. After the reporting, it said the "outside financial interests and activities of Onerep's CEO do not align with our values," and ultimately wound the partnership down - discontinuing Monitor Plus entirely on 17 December 2025. When a company as privacy-focused as Mozilla walks away, that's a strong signal.

It's also worth noting OneRep is registered as a Virginia LLC but, per the same reporting, operates out of Belarus and Cyprus - which muddies exactly where your data is processed and under whose jurisdiction.

In fairness to OneRep

We try to report the whole picture. OneRep did respond publicly rather than go silent, its CEO gave an on-the-record explanation, and the company achieved SOC 2 Type II compliance in July 2025. Many users report that the removals and monthly re-scans function fine for them, and there's no evidence of a breach of OneRep's own systems. None of that resolves the core conflict of interest, which is structural rather than a one-off - but you should know the service isn't a scam that takes your money and does nothing. It's a functioning service with a disqualifying ownership problem.

Does it actually work?

There's no independent, controlled measurement: OneRep wasn't part of Consumer Reports' study. Its marketed coverage of "310+ sites" is inflated by counting mirror sites of the same underlying databases - an independent analysis put the real unique-broker count closer to 210. Some users also report hard-to-cancel billing and slow or partial removals, though that appears to be a minority pattern rather than the norm.
