Privacy Bee review

Orange - trade-offs Reviewed July 2026

Independent, founder-owned (Harry Maugans) · Alpharetta, US · since 2020

Bottom line

Privacy Bee has real things going for it: it's bootstrapped and founder-owned (no VC or conglomerate), a PCMag Editors' Choice in 2024 and 2025, offers a free exposure scan, and states it doesn't sell your data. Two things hold it back from green. First, the founder's previous company was an ad-intent data business he sold to a large B2B data aggregator - a personal "both ends" history worth knowing, even though it's not a current corporate tie. Second, its advertised coverage is enormous but an independent long-term reviewer found only a fraction of it actually populated. There's also no independent effectiveness test.

Who owns it - and can you trust them?

Privacy Bee is bootstrapped and owned by its founder-CEO, Harry Maugans, with no outside investors - which, on its face, is a good sign: there's no VC or data conglomerate pulling strings. We found no evidence the current company operates or is a sibling of any data broker.

The founder's ad-data past

Before Privacy Bee, Maugans founded and grew Clickagy, a behavioral/"intent-data" ad-tech firm that harvested browsing and keyword-to-device signals, and sold it to ZoomInfo - a large B2B contact-data aggregator - in October 2020. There's no current corporate link between ZoomInfo and Privacy Bee, so this is history, not an active conflict. But given that the whole pitch of this category is "we're on the opposite side from the data industry," a founder who built and sold into that industry is context you deserve.

One more nuance, so we're accurate: an independent long-term reviewer noted Privacy Bee is not BBB-accredited, but BBB nonetheless gives it an A+ rating - those are two different things, and we don't count non-accreditation against it.

Does it actually work?

Privacy Bee wasn't part of Consumer Reports' controlled study, so there's no independent, rigorous measurement of its removal rate - PCMag's Editors' Choice awards are positive but editorial, not a lab test. What independent scrutiny exists is cautionary: CyberInsider, running the paid service over six months, reported that only around 511 sites were actually populated in their dashboard, against marketing that touts 1,000+ (and a "181,048 sites" figure). Treat the headline coverage numbers as aspirational, not delivered.

Your privacy & data

On the plus side, Privacy Bee doesn't require your SSN, date of birth, or a photo, and it states it doesn't sell personal data. The wrinkles are in the fine print: its policy reserves the right to use aggregated or de-identified data "for any purpose, including research and marketing," it allows third-party advertising cookies on its own marketing site, and it retains your data for up to a year after you leave (longer if legally required). None of that is unusual, but it's a step less clean than the minimalist independents.

A 2021 complaint, in context

In 2021, an Indian privacy advocate (Naavi.org) published an open-letter complaint alleging Privacy Bee sent aggressive, legal-sounding CCPA/GDPR deletion-demand emails to companies. It was a single advocate's allegation, not a regulatory finding or court case, and we present it as exactly that - but it's part of the public record and speaks to how the service pursues removals on your behalf.

Security

Privacy Bee prominently claims AICPA SOC 2 Type II certification. As with most of the category the underlying audit isn't public (normal), so it's effectively self-reported; there's no ISO 27001 claim, and we found no breach history. An adequate posture, but less independently evidenced than DeleteMe's.
