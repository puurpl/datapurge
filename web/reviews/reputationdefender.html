ReputationDefender review

Red — avoid Reviewed July 2026

Owned by Gen Digital (Norton / Avast / LifeLock) · since 2006

Bottom line

ReputationDefender is a big, financially stable name — but for data-broker removal specifically, the evidence is damning. In Consumer Reports' controlled 2024 study, it removed just 6% of test data within four months, near the bottom of everything tested. Its pricing is opaque (you have to take a sales call), it draws complaints about hard-sell tactics and cancellation, and its ultimate parent, Gen Digital, owns Avast — a brand the FTC banned in 2024 from selling browsing data. When a service barely works and sits inside a group with that track record, our advice is simple: skip it. Do the removals yourself for free, or pick one of our green options.

Does it actually work?

This is the heart of the red rating. ReputationDefender was one of the services Consumer Reports put through its 2024 controlled test (28 volunteers, 13 major people-search sites, tracked over four months), and it removed just 6% of exposures — one of the two worst results in the study. For context, volunteers doing opt-outs manually and for free hit around 70%. You would very likely get dramatically more of your data removed by spending an afternoon with a free tool than by paying ReputationDefender.

There's also a value trap in the pricing: the CR-tested tier was the low-cost ~$99/year "Privacy Pro," and reviewers note the far pricier executive tiers ($1,000–$10,000/year) are where the real service lives — so most ordinary buyers are getting the version that scored 6%.

Who owns it — and can you trust them?

ReputationDefender was founded in 2006, bought by NortonLifeLock in 2021, and now sits inside Gen Digital (the company formed when NortonLifeLock merged with Avast), whose brand family spans Norton, Avast, LifeLock, Avira, AVG, and CCleaner. ReputationDefender itself isn't a data broker. But the parent's record on personal data is exactly what a privacy-conscious buyer should weigh:

Sibling brand Avast was FTC-banned for selling browsing data

In 2024 the FTC ordered Avast — now part of the same Gen Digital family — to pay $16.5 million and permanently banned it from selling or licensing browsing data for advertising, after its Jumpshot subsidiary sold detailed consumers' browsing data to more than 100 third parties from 2014 to 2020, all while Avast marketed itself as blocking online tracking. That's not ReputationDefender, but it's the company you'd be handing money and data to at the group level.

ReputationDefender's own privacy policy also permits sharing your data across parent, subsidiaries, and joint ventures, and with third-party advertising partners for personalised advertising — broader than a pure opt-out tool needs — and it retains your data for seven years after you leave.

The sales experience

Because there's no transparent price list, the funnel starts with a "free scan" that, per numerous 2023–2024 BBB and Trustpilot complaints, leads to aggressive sales calls, reports some customers call inaccurate, and difficulty cancelling (with cancellation gated behind an assigned rep). On the plus side, it does not collect your SSN or government-ID numbers — a genuine point in its favour — but it doesn't outweigh a 6% removal rate and an opaque, hard-sell purchase.
