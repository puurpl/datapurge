IDX review

Orange - trade-offs Reviewed July 2026

Identity Theft Guard Solutions, Inc. · owned by Kingswood Capital · since 2003

Bottom line

IDX is a serious, long-running identity-protection firm - the kind governments and Fortune 500s hire after a breach - and on ownership it's clean: no data-broker conflict, human-assisted removal, and it was rated a mid-level performer in Consumer Reports' 2024 test. The catches are practical: it wants your Social Security Number for its core monitoring, its ownership has changed hands three times in under three years, and its data-removal product ("ForgetMe") is really one feature of a broader identity-theft suite rather than a focused removal tool.

Who owns it - and can you trust them?

IDX (legal name Identity Theft Guard Solutions, Inc.) has been around since 2003 and built its reputation on enterprise and government breach-response work - it's provided identity protection to victims of major incidents like the 2015 OPM breach. Its ownership, though, has been a revolving door lately: independent until 2022, then acquired by the threat-intelligence firm ZeroFox, then - after ZeroFox itself was taken private - divested in November 2024 to Kingswood Capital Management, a Los Angeles private-equity firm. So, despite what older articles say, IDX is not currently a "ZeroFox company." (It's also unrelated to the similarly-named IdentityForce, which is a TransUnion brand.)

No "both ends" conflict - but note the churn

Kingswood's disclosed portfolio is a grab-bag of unrelated businesses with no data-broker, people-search, or ad-data component, so there's no "fox guarding the henhouse" problem here. The flag is different: three owners in under three years introduces real uncertainty about who will control your data - and what commitments they'll keep - a year or two from now.

Does it actually work?

IDX was one of the seven services Consumer Reports put through its 2024 controlled study, and it came out a mid-level performer - broadly comparable to DeleteMe and Kanary. That's meaningful: unlike several rivals, IDX has some independent effectiveness data, and it's middling rather than poor. Its human-assisted "ForgetMe" process is a genuine plus for harder removals, though IDX quotes its own coverage inconsistently (100+ in some places, 200+ in others).

Your privacy & data

This is the main trade-off: IDX is built around identity-theft monitoring, so it asks for your Social Security Number as a baseline, and its dark-web add-ons collect financial account numbers. If you only want off people-search sites, that's more sensitive data than you strictly need to hand over. On the plus side, its policy commits to not selling or sharing with non-affiliated companies for marketing without consent - tempered by the usual carve-outs for credit bureaus, the entity that paid for your plan, and service providers. US-hosted, US-focused.

Security

IDX states SOC 2 compliance (corroborated by an independent security-profile aggregator) and claims ISO 27001/9001/27701 certifications, though we couldn't independently reach the certificate page to confirm the specifics. We found no evidence of an IDX breach. One consumer-safety note: scammers have circulated fake "IDX data breach notification" letters impersonating the brand - not IDX's fault, but verify any such communication before acting on it.

Pros

  • No data-broker conflict; owner's portfolio is clean
  • Has independent (mid-level) Consumer Reports data
  • Human-assisted "ForgetMe" removals
  • Deep enterprise/government track record

Cons

  • Requires SSN for core monitoring
  • Three owners in under three years
  • Removal is one feature of a bigger, pricier suite
  • Support/cancellation complaints; inconsistent coverage figures
