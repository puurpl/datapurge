Paid removal services, reviewed

DataPurge is free and always will be. But if you'd rather pay someone to do the opt-outs and keep watch for you, some services are worth it - and some are the very thing you're trying to escape. Here's an honest, source-backed look at who owns each one, whether they can be trusted with your data, and whether they actually work.

💰

You don't have to pay anyone

Every removal these services perform, you can do yourself for free with DataPurge and, if you're in California, DROP. In fact, when Consumer Reports tested this whole industry in 2024, people doing opt-outs manually removed more of their data (~70%) than any paid service managed. What you're really buying from a paid service is time and ongoing monitoring - not magic. If a service ever seems to promise more than that, be skeptical.

How this page pays for itself - and why that doesn't buy a good review

Some of the links below are affiliate links. Signing up through one earns DataPurge a commission, at no extra cost to you. An affiliate link buys a coffee, not your trust. A link signifies no partnership, endorsement, or special consideration - the ratings reflect what the public record shows, link or not. Any button that earns a commission is marked with an affiliate tag. If the evidence changes, the ranking changes - commission or not.

What the colours mean

Green

Trustworthy ownership, sound data handling, and credible evidence it works. Worth using, and safe to recommend to family.

Orange

Legitimate and usable, but with real trade-offs - weak proof of effectiveness, a data-hungry sign-up, or an ownership wrinkle. Read the review before you buy.

Red

Serious, well-documented concerns - a proven conflict of interest, or it simply doesn't work. Best avoided, with the reasons and sources laid out.

Discontinued: Mozilla Monitor Plus

You may see Mozilla Monitor Plus recommended elsewhere - but Mozilla shut down the paid removal service on 17 December 2025, deleted all removal data, and refunded subscribers. The removals had been run by OneRep, and Mozilla ended the relationship after the conflict-of-interest reporting (see the OneRep review for the full story). The free Mozilla Monitor breach-alert tool lives on and is worth using - it just no longer removes you from data brokers.

How each service is reviewed

Every rating starts from the public record, approached from the reader's side rather than the industry's. Each service is thoroughly researched online before anything lands on this page: the legal entity and its ultimate owner are traced - including whether that owner also profits from selling personal data; the privacy policy is read closely for what you must hand over and whether it can be shared or sold; retention terms and data jurisdiction are noted; security posture, certifications, and breach history are checked; 2026 pricing and plan tiers are confirmed at the source; and - most important - any independent, controlled evidence that the service actually works is weighed. The single best source there is Consumer Reports' 2024 study, which is leaned on heavily and cited throughout.

Each of those findings is then checked against primary and reputable secondary sources, and every review links them so the reasoning is easy to verify. Ratings weigh ownership and the “both ends” question most heavily, then privacy and data handling, then proven effectiveness. Companies change and errors happen - anything that looks wrong can be flagged via a GitHub issue and it will be corrected. More services, and more affiliate options where they exist, will be added over time; the presence or absence of a commission never moves a rating.
